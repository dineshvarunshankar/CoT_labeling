# thermal

## Human Findings

### 001 - Thermal imagery emphasizes heat contrast

Thermal images emphasize heat contrast rather than color or fine clothing
detail. Body heat can make a casualty readable against cooler ground, but hot
ground, sun-warmed surfaces, smoke, dust, and low contrast can make boundaries
ambiguous.

### 002 - Thermal reasoning should not use RGB-only cues

Do not rely on RGB-only evidence such as blood color or clothing color in pure
thermal imagery. Explain thermal evidence in terms of shape, contrast, body
heat, missing or continuous warm regions, and nearby warm or cool objects.

### 003 - Thermal blood assessment needs baseline contrast

Compare suspected blood or wound regions against the subject's torso as the warm
body baseline and nearby ground as the ambient baseline. Bright or dark regions
are useful only when their shape and location plausibly connect to the subject.

### 004 - Cooling blood can appear darker than the body

In thermal imagery, possible blood pooling may appear as an irregular dark or
grey organic-shaped pool or trail near the body, especially if it is cooler than
the torso but still spatially connected to the subject.

### 005 - Thermal image quality can force hard reasoning

Saturation, smoke, dust, fog, sun-warmed ground, low contrast, or small subject
scale can hide limb boundaries and body contact points. only mark such cases as hard
and explain the visual limitation directly.

### 006 - Check contrastive thermal flow around the subject

For any category, inspect contrastive thermal flow around the boxed subject:
irregular bright, dark, or grey trails, spreading regions, or directional
patterns that may indicate warm or cooling blood/fluid, showing where the
flow originated or where it is heading. Treat this as supporting evidence 
only when the shape, direction, and contact point plausibly connect to
the boxed subject and to a casualty abnormality such as a wound-like contrast,
amputation or abrupt limb termination, unusual body contact with the ground, or
other injury-looking posture. Do not rely on flow-like contrast alone when it may
belong to nearby people, objects, shadows, hot ground, or sensor artifacts.
